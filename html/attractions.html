What to do

Explore the seemingly endless wonders of Japan

Osaka Castle

Osaka Castle, or Osaka-jo in Japanese, is one of the most famous landmarks in Japan. This five-story castle is among Japan's most visually stunning marvels in the country with a rich history dating back 450 years. The surrounding parks and gardens help create a sense of natural wonder and beauty in the area. Osaka Castle Park was opened in 1931, and is famous for its changing foliage, from the sakura or cherry blossom season in the spring, to the koyo or falling leaves season in the autumn.

  • Admission fee: ￥600 ($5.66) | Free entry for children 15 years and under
  • Age range: All ages

Dotonbori

As an essential taste of Japan's nightlife, Dotonbori is a vibrant neon cityscape with dozens of shops, restaurants, street vendors and clubs. The origins of the Dotonbori area date back to the early 1600's! The Dotanbori river had its banks expanded by a local businessman in hopes of increasing commercial oppurtunities. Within 50 years, the area erupted in popularity. Now the entertainment hub, also know as "Japan's Broadway," continues to attract locals and travelers alike. There is also a river cruise during the daytime!

  • River cruise pricing: General admission ¥900 ($8.51) | Children ¥400 ($3.78)
  • Age range: 6 and up

Universal Studios, Japan

Universal Studios Japan is a theme park similar to Universal Orlando Resort in that it contains selected attractions from its American counterpart (such as the Harry Potter and the Forbidden Journey attraction). However, Universal Japan has its own unique must-see attractions, such as Despicable Me Minion Mayhem, The Amazing Adventures of Spiderman, and many more!

Shop in Shinsaibashi

Shinsaibashi-Suji is the best-known shopping area in Osaka. With a rich history spanning over 380 years, it has been an established hub of commerce since the Edo Period. From traditional kimono tailors and western clothing, to restaurants and jewelers, the choices and possibilites to shop are virtually endless!

  • Prices may vary
  • All ages are welcome

Shintennoji Temple

Shintennoji temple was one of the first Buddhist temples built in Japan, and it still stands to this day. Located near Shintennojimae-yuhigaoka Station and Tennoji Station, it is very accessible to travelers. If you have an interest in Japanese history, architecture, or Buddhist culture, this is a must-see! Believed to be built about 1,400 years ago, this temple is rich in history. Originally constructed in 593 AD, the temple was created to teach and spread Buddhism at a time when it wasn't particularly popular. In 1946 Shintennoji was home to an independent sect of Buddhists, whose goal was to revolutionize the very concept of "sects" in and of itself; they wanted to allow any and everyone to join. Additionally, there are several different buildings, gardens, and pagodas on the temple grounds that you can visit.

  • Prices for entry vary because of multiple attractions
  • All ages welcome

Sumo Wrestling

Sumo wrestling is the national sport of Japan. A style of wrestling dating back to ancient times, the sport was originally seen as a performance to entertain Shinto gods (also known as kami), which are sacred spirits believed to take the form of different elements, such as wind, rain, trees, rivers, etc. Many rituals with religious backgrounds, such as "purifying" the ring with salt, are still followed to this day. The rules of sumo are as follows: The first wrestler to exit the ring or touch the ground with any body part other than the soles of their feet loses. Matches take place on an elevated ring called the dohyo, which is made of clay and covered in a layer of sand. A round usually lasts just a few seconds and rarely goes over a minute or two. There are no weight restrictions or classes in sumo, allowing for easily mismatched line-ups; however, if one's technique and skill is great enough, they can still be victorious!

  • Age range: 4 and up
